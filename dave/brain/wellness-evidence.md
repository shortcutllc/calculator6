# What the evidence actually says about corporate wellness

**Read this before making ANY efficacy claim in copy, content, a pitch or a conference talk.**

Researched 2026-07-21 after Will's instruction to stop treating two speakers' views as the
room's consensus. Every claim below carries the URL it was read on. This file exists because
Dave built a Goal 3 thesis ("stop measuring by ROI, measure whether anyone shows up") that does
not survive contact with the literature, and nearly took it to the exact audiences most likely
to know that.

---

## THE FINDING THAT KILLED THE THESIS

**William Fleming, Wellbeing Research Centre, University of Oxford.** N = 46,336 workers across
233 UK organisations. He compared people who **participated** in workplace wellbeing
interventions (resilience training, mindfulness, wellbeing apps) against non-participants at the
same employers. Verbatim from the abstract: *"Across multiple subjective well-being indicators,
participants appear no better off."*
https://wellbeing.hmc.ox.ac.uk/publications/employee-well-being-outcomes-from-individual-level-mental-health-interventions-cross-sectional-evidence-from-the-united-kingdom/
https://wellbeing.hmc.ox.ac.uk/news/more-ambition-needed-to-improve-workplace-wellbeing/

**Showing up was the exposure variable. Showing up did not correlate with being better off.**
That is a direct hit on any "participation proves it works" argument.

Fair caveats, which matter: it is **cross-sectional, not causal**, and Fleming himself
interrogates selection bias. He did not test organisational interventions. He explicitly said
*"if employees do want access to mindfulness apps and sleep programs... there is not anything
wrong with that."* He is not saying don't offer things.

## THE TWO BIG NULL RCTs — and they were NOT app studies

We cannot dodge these by saying "that research was about apps."

**Illinois Workplace Wellness Study** (Jones, Molitor & Reif, *QJE* 2019). RCT, ~4,800
employees, onsite screening plus in-person activities. Raised screening rates and shifted health
beliefs slightly. **No detectable effect** on medical spending, health behaviours, self-reported
health, biometrics or productivity at 12, 24 or 30 months. Confidence intervals tight enough to
rule out ~84% of prior published savings estimates.
https://www.nber.org/programs-projects/projects-and-centers/workplace-wellness/illinois-workplace-wellness-results
https://ssir.org/articles/entry/ineffective_workplace_wellness
**The detail that matters most to us: participants had already spent about $115/month LESS on
healthcare in the year BEFORE the programme started, and were already exercising.**

**Song & Baicker, BJ's Wholesale** (*JAMA* 2019; 3-year follow-up *Health Affairs* 2021).
33,000+ employees, 160 worksites, **in-person, dietitian-led** modules at the worksite. Better
self-reported behaviours; **no significant difference** in health, clinical markers, spending,
absenteeism, tenure or job performance — at 18 months and again at three years.
https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2730614 · https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34097526/

**Al Lewis & Vik Khanna** — the methodological critique: *"It is not possible to 'match'
motivated volunteers with non-motivated non-volunteers, because of the unobservable variable of
willingness to engage."* Documented a programme where participants outperformed non-participants
**before it began**. https://www.insurancethoughtleadership.com/life-health/workplace-wellness-shows-no-savings

**The healthy adherer effect**: in a BMJ meta-analysis, good adherence to **placebo** was
associated with roughly 44% lower mortality. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16809709/
High engagement with a nothing is a documented phenomenon.

## THE OTHER SIDE — but read what it actually supports

- **Ron Goetzel** (Johns Hopkins) defends the category, but his argument is largely "the
  treatment was underdosed," not "here is a positive RCT." https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32077304/
- **Jan-Emmanuel De Neve** (Oxford Saïd) has the strongest pro-wellbeing evidence: measured
  employee wellbeing predicts profitability, valuation and stock performance across ~1,600 US
  firms; a one-point happiness rise associated with ~12% higher weekly sales at BT call centres.
  https://wellbeing.hmc.ox.ac.uk/workplace/ **But this shows wellbeing correlates with
  performance, NOT that wellbeing PROGRAMMES produce wellbeing.** De Neve and Fleming are at the
  same centre and are not in conflict.
- **Deloitte UK's £4.70 per £1** is the most-quoted number in the market and it is **a literature
  review of 26 sources, not primary trial data.** Skeptics know this.
  https://www.deloitte.com/uk/en/about/press-room/poor-mental-health-costs-uk-employers-51-billion-a-year-for-employees.html
- **RAND/Mattke on PepsiCo**: disease management returned ~$3.78 per $1; **lifestyle management
  did not return more than it cost.** https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_briefs/RB9744.html
- **Gallup (Harter)**: engagement and work environment drive wellbeing more than programmes;
  "workplace policies and benefits alone are insufficient to improve health outcomes."

## THE MOST HONEST POSITION AVAILABLE TO US
**Jeff Levin-Scherz** (WTW; Harvard Chan) — traditional wellness doesn't move health outcomes,
but employers should reshape the environment, and they gain an edge from healthier, happier
staff **even if claims costs don't fall.**
https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/benefits-compensation/wellness-programs-value-disputed-defended

## ⚠️ WHO ARGUES PARTICIPATION IS THE RIGHT MEASURE? ALMOST ONLY VENDORS.
Henry Albrecht (Limeade), Danna Korn (Sonic Boom), Scott Rotermund (Welltok) — all vendors.
HERO's quantitative version measures **perceived** effectiveness self-reported by the employer,
which is a self-assessment loop, not an outcome.
**No peer-reviewed source was found arguing participation is a sufficient measure of value.**
And **Grokker, a fellow wellbeing vendor, argues the opposite**: their "participation paradox"
is that superusers are the already-healthy majority, so participation metrics **mask** failure
and create "a false sense of success."
https://www.grokker.com/blog/the-wellbeing-program-participation-paradox-how-to-reach-your-hardest-to-engage-employees

## Modest support for our actual modalities (weak evidence, state it honestly)
Network meta-analysis, 15 trials / 688 healthcare workers: yoga (SMD −0.71) and **massage
(SMD −0.43)** reduced occupational stress vs control. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8263904/
These are small, short-horizon, subjective-outcome trials against passive controls. They support
"a massage makes people feel less stressed that week." They do **not** support "wellbeing
programmes change organisational outcomes."

# WHAT SHORTCUT MAY AND MAY NOT CLAIM

**MAY NOT CLAIM** — indefensible, and the people we most want to impress will know it:
- That wellness reduces healthcare cost, absenteeism or turnover.
- Any ROI multiple. Not Deloitte's £4.70, not Aldana's $3.50.
- That high participation proves the programme works.
- That our 90%/87% figures are evidence of employee outcomes.

**MAY CLAIM** — true, verifiable, and it is what the spine already said:
- **"You're already paying for wellness your team doesn't use. We're the part they actually show
  up for."** This is a claim about **WASTE, not EFFICACY**, and it is airtight. Nobody disputes
  that most wellness spend goes unused; our attendance data proves ours doesn't.
- 90%+ of slots booked, 87% rebook, 500+ companies, the named clients — as facts about **our
  service being used and repurchased**, never as proof of health outcomes.
- Zero lift, one vendor, whole team. Operational claims, fully defensible.

**The distinction in one line: we sell a wellness budget that doesn't get wasted. We do not sell
a health outcome.**

# THE THREE ATTACKS A SHARP SKEPTIC WILL MAKE
1. **Booking IS the selection bias.** A 90% fill rate says motivated, already-healthier employees
   found their way to a nice thing. Illinois participants were $115/month cheaper *beforehand*.
2. **The one study that measured our exact metric found participants no better off** (Fleming).
   And rebooking is a satisfaction signal — Song & Baicker's employees changed behaviour for
   three years with zero outcome change. Liking a thing is fully compatible with it doing nothing.
3. **Utilisation is a supply-side metric measuring our operations, not the client's return.**
   Halve the slots and hit 100%. And 87% rebooking is a **procurement** metric — it measures the
   HR buyer's satisfaction and budget inertia, not employee outcomes.

# THE OPPORTUNITY HIDING IN THE CRITIQUE
What would actually answer the skeptics: a **waitlist or staggered-rollout comparison**,
outcomes measured on **everyone eligible** rather than just attendees, and turnover and absence
pulled from the client's **HRIS** rather than our booking system. Stepped-wedge designs are
feasible at our scale. **Shortcut runs events at 500+ companies and could actually do this. No
vendor in this category has.** That is a far stronger Goal 3 asset than publishing participation
statistics, and it would make Will the only operator in the field with real evidence.
