# Voice samples — notes Will approved, and why they work

Concrete examples beat rules. When drafting, read these before reaching for the rulebook: the
rules describe the sound, the samples ARE the sound. Add one every time Will approves or
rewrites something, with the reasoning attached. Never add an unapproved draft.

---

## SAMPLE 1 — warm re-open on a live deal that went quiet
**Beverly ("Bev") Marsters, OpenSesame · 2026-07-21 · approved by Will**

> Subject: Re: Shortcut Next Steps
>
> Hey Bev,
>
> Hope your summer's going well. It's been a few weeks since I sent the Portland proposal over,
> and I know you said the first event was a longer decision, so there's no pressure from me
> here.
>
> The one piece with any clock on it is the September event in Texas. If that's still something
> you're considering, we'd want to start locking dates fairly soon to get the right Pros booked
> for it. Everything else can sit as long as you need.
>
> Is Texas still on the table, or has the thinking moved since we spoke?
>
> Thanks!
>
> Will

### Why it works

- **It has exactly one reason to exist.** September has a staffing clock. Everything else is
  explicitly parked. A note with one honest reason beats a note with three manufactured ones.
- **It removes pressure out loud** ("no pressure from me here", "can sit as long as you need").
  With an overwhelmed buyer, de-risking outperforms urgency (FOMU > FOMO).
- **It does not re-pitch.** She had already taken a call and read two proposals. No new links,
  no proof points, no service list. A receipt would have insulted her.
- **It uses her own words back at her.** "A longer decision" is Will's own Jun 1 phrasing of
  what she told him. Nothing asserted that she didn't say.
- **One question, and it offers her the exit** ("or has the thinking moved"). Easy to answer
  "not yet" without ending anything.
- **Name discipline: "Bev", not "Beverly".** She signed her reply "Bev" and Will had already
  switched. Reverting to the formal name would have read colder than the relationship is.
  **Always check how they signed off before choosing a name.**

### Mechanics
110 words · one question · no dashes as punctuation · one exclamation mark, sign-off only ·
varied sentence length · no banned words · every fact traceable to the thread.

## SAMPLE 2 — warm first follow-up after meeting in person
**Marissa Reyes, Friends of the High Line · 2026-07-21 · register dictated by Will**

> Hey Marissa,
>
> It's been a few months, so this one's well overdue. I really enjoyed meeting you at Workhuman
> and I hope the rest of the conference was good to you. Hope you enjoyed the massage too.
>
> I've been thinking about what you told me. Your team heads across the street for massage,
> you've already got Cigna dollars going toward wellness, and you've got a committee internally
> who cares about this. That's most of the way there.
>
> I'd still love to talk about bringing it into your offices instead, and it can usually be
> paid out of those same Cigna dollars.
>
> If you've got any time this summer I'd love to catch up properly. And if now isn't right,
> just say so and I'll try you later in the year.
>
> Cheers!
>
> Will

### Why it works

- **It spends words on being human before getting to business.** Owns the gap ("well overdue"),
  says he enjoyed meeting her, asks how the rest of her conference went, references the massage
  she actually booked at our booth. Dave's first attempt was 95 tight words and Will rejected
  it: **terseness is not warmth, and compression reads as automated** (Sivers: don't be too
  succinct).
- **It reflects her own situation back as a list she recognises** — massage across the street,
  Cigna dollars, internal committee — then lands the dry understated line: "That's most of the
  way there." That sentence could not appear in any other note Will sends.
- **The ask is soft and seasonal** ("any time this summer"), with a graceful out attached.

### The register rule this sample encodes
**Cold notes stay tight. Warm notes breathe.** Someone Will physically met gets a person
picking up a conversation, not an operator executing a follow-up.

## Open rule question (awaiting Will)
Sample 2 uses **"I'd love to" twice**, which is on Dave's banned list. The ban looks
over-broad: filler in a cold note to a stranger, ordinary human speech in a warm note to
someone you met. **Proposed: scope the ban to cold/first-touch copy only.** Not applied
anywhere else until Will rules.
