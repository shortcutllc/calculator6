# Shortcut — Copywriting Brief

## Company Overview

Shortcut is an all-in-one corporate wellness platform. We bring in-person wellness experiences directly into offices: chair massage, headshots, hair and grooming, nails, facials, and mindfulness workshops. One vendor handles everything. 500+ companies nationwide trust us, from DraftKings and Wix to BCG, PwC, Paramount, and White & Case.

**What makes us different:** We're not a wellness app, a meditation subscription, or a benefits vendor with a slide deck. We physically show up. Our team arrives at your office with equipment, sets up in 20 minutes, and your employees walk away feeling better. No admin burden on the client. No logistics headaches. One vendor, one invoice, every service you need.

**Who we sell to:** People Ops leaders, HR directors, CHROs, VPs of Employee Experience at mid-market and enterprise companies (typically 500+ employees). They're exhausted by "wellness theater" — pizza parties, stress balls, meditation app licenses nobody uses. They want something that actually works, that employees actually want, and that doesn't create more work for them.

**Our tagline:** Employee Happiness Delivered.

**Website:** getshortcut.co

## Brand Voice Guide

### Voice
Calm, human, practical. Like a competent friend who happens to run a wellness company. Dry humor when it fits. Self-aware. Never trying too hard to be funny. We position against corporate wellness cliches without being cynical. We state what we do plainly. The service sells itself. Confident but never loud. We don't need to convince you wellness matters. You already know. We just make it effortless.

### The line we walk
Shortcut exists in the gap between "spa luxury" and "corporate vendor." The copy should feel premium without being pretentious, professional without being stiff. Think: the best hotel you've stayed at. Nobody oversold you on the lobby. It just felt right.

### Patterns in the good copy
- Short declarative sentences
- Juxtaposition (familiar corporate thing vs. what we actually do)
- Double meanings ("recharging" = phone + person)
- No setup, just the punch
- Reads like something you'd say, not something you'd write

### Writing rules
- NEVER use dashes as punctuation. No em dashes, en dashes, or hyphens between clauses. Write clean sentences. Period. New sentence.
- Never use: "elevate", "leverage", "synergy", "unlock", "empower", "transform", "reimagine", "seamless", "holistic", "curated"
- Never start with "In today's..." or "At Shortcut, we believe..."
- No exclamation points to manufacture energy
- Don't rhyme. Don't force alliteration.
- No buzzwords. If a McKinsey deck would use the word, we don't.
- "Wellness" is fine. "Wellness journey" is not.
- Specifics over superlatives. "15 minutes" beats "quick." "500+ companies" beats "many."
- If a sentence works for any wellness company, rewrite it until it only works for Shortcut.

### What Shortcut copy is NOT
- It's not a wellness brand that talks about "journeys" and "mindful spaces"
- It's not a tech startup that says "we're disrupting corporate wellness"
- It's not a luxury spa that says "indulge in pure relaxation"
- It's not a benefits vendor that leads with ROI stats and engagement metrics
- It's none of these because we're all of them, simplified into one sentence

### Tone by context

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Proposal / client-facing | Warm, professional, confident. Let the service details do the work. |
| Conference / event signage | Punchy, dry wit, maximum impact in minimum words. |
| Landing page hero | Direct benefit statement. No preamble. |
| Email outreach | Conversational, peer-to-peer. Not salesy. |
| Internal / sales enablement | Practical, no-nonsense, data-forward. |
| Social media | Human, slightly playful, never corporate. |

### Process for producing copy
1. Start from the insight: what does the reader already believe, and what do we want them to feel?
2. Write 3 directions: safe, medium, brave. Label them.
3. Keep headlines under 10 words. Body copy sentences under 20.
4. Read it out loud. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it.
5. Flag which direction you'd fight for and why.

### 3. Pre-Event Outreach Emails (3 directions)

**Context:** Cold outreach to Workhuman Live attendees. The email offers early access to a massage booking in exchange for a 10-minute conversation. The meeting is framed as "before or after your session" — no extra calendar block. The massage is the gift, the meeting is the soft ask.

**Direction 1 — Safe:**
Subject: "Early access: Recharge Lounge at Workhuman"
> Hi [First Name], Will Newton, Shortcut. We bring chair massage and wellness programming into offices. We're running a Recharge Lounge at Workhuman in the Gratitude Garden. 15-minute massage sessions between keynotes. We're opening booking to all conference attendees closer to the event, but I wanted to give you first access before that goes out. Book a slot and I'll meet you there 10 minutes before or after your session. No extra calendar hold. Just a quick conversation about what wellness looks like at [Company] while you're already in the Garden.

**Direction 2 — Medium (Recommended):**
Subject: "You're getting this before 3,000 people"
> [First Name], Will Newton, Shortcut. We do in-office wellness. Massage, headshots, grooming. One vendor, no hassle. We're running a Recharge Lounge at Workhuman. 15-minute chair massages in the Gratitude Garden. Booking opens to all 3,000+ attendees closer to the event. This is early access. Grab a slot and I'll meet you right before or after your session. 10 minutes to swap notes on what wellness could look like at [Company]. No separate meeting, no extra calendar block.

**Direction 3 — Brave:**
Subject: "10 minutes of your time, 15 on your shoulders"
> [First Name], Will Newton, Shortcut. We bring massage and wellness into offices. That's the whole pitch. We're running a recharge lounge at Workhuman. 15-minute chair massages, Gratitude Garden. Booking goes out to the full conference soon. You're getting early access. Book a slot. I'll meet you 10 minutes before or after. One conversation about how [Company] handles wellness, no extra time out of your day.

### 5. Standalone Proposals (client-facing)

**Context:** PDF/web proposals sent to prospects after an initial call. Pricing details, service descriptions, and logistics. Tone is warm and professional. The service details do the selling.

**Key copy patterns in proposals:**
- Service names are straightforward: "Chair Massage Event", "Corporate Headshot Event", "Hair & Styling Event"
- No flowery descriptions. State what happens: "Our expert therapists create a spa-like ambiance with soothing scents, customized lighting and relaxing sounds."
- Logistics are spelled out plainly: setup time, equipment provided, space requirements
- Pricing is transparent: hourly rates, number of pros, total cost. No hidden fees language.
